# CERT-EKS-000035 — MOTS-c Exercise and Age-Related Physical Decline

**Root asset:** `CERT-PKS-000006`  
**Version:** 1.0.0  
**Status:** CLOSED FOR CORE PRIMARY FULL-TEXT EXTRACTION  
**Last Review:** 2026-07-16

## Publication

**Title:** MOTS-c is an exercise-induced mitochondrial-encoded regulator of age-dependent physical decline and muscle homeostasis  
**Journal:** Nature Communications  
**Year:** 2021  
**DOI:** `10.1038/s41467-020-20790-0`  
**PMID:** `None`

## Study design

Open-access translational study combining human exercise sampling, mouse intervention experiments and cell models.

## Population or model

Ten healthy young men in the exercise-expression component; young, middle-aged and old mice; C2C12 cells.

## Intervention or exposure

Human exercise sampling measured endogenous MOTS-c. Exogenous MOTS-c intervention outcomes were tested primarily in mice and cells.

> This is a historical study-design record, not a recommended administration protocol.

## Extracted outcomes

- In the ten-man human exercise component, skeletal-muscle MOTS-c rose 11.9-fold after exercise and circulating levels rose about 1.5–1.6-fold during/after exercise.
- Exogenous MOTS-c improved multiple physical-performance outcomes in mouse models.
- Old treated mice ran approximately twofold longer and 2.16-fold farther in one reported experiment.
- Late-life intermittent treatment improved several healthspan measures in mice.
- Overall lifespan results were not statistically conclusive; the overall curve was reported as p=0.23.
- The human component measured endogenous response, not therapeutic efficacy of injected MOTS-c.

## Limitations

- Human sample was small and observational with respect to endogenous exercise response.
- Treatment efficacy was established in mice, not humans.
- Multiple animal doses and models do not create a human protocol.
- Longevity findings require larger cohorts.

## Permitted claims

- Exercise increased endogenous MOTS-c in a small human study.
- Exogenous MOTS-c improved physical-capacity outcomes in several mouse models.
- The study did not establish injected MOTS-c efficacy in humans.

## Prohibited claims

- Human exercise-performance enhancement claims from injections.
- Human longevity claims.
- Conversion of mouse regimens into human dosing.
